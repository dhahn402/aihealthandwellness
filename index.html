Anchor Topic

What is a sound bed?

A sound bed — also called a vibroacoustic table or lounge — is a padded bed or recliner with speakers called transducers built right into it. Instead of only hearing music, you also feel it: the transducers turn low-frequency sound into gentle vibration you sense through your whole body. People often describe it as lying inside the music. You'll find them in spas, wellness studios, and sound-healing sessions as a way to relax and unwind.

How it works

Sound beds mostly use low tones — commonly around 30 to 120 Hz — frequencies you feel more than you hear, like the deep thump of a bass speaker. Those vibrations reach the body through the same touch-sensing nerves you use to feel a hug or a heartbeat. Many designs pair the vibration with matching sound through headphones, so what you hear and what you feel line up into one immersive, calming experience.

A note on the science

Early research is encouraging but still limited. Small studies suggest that vibroacoustic sessions can nudge the body toward its natural "rest" state and lower physical signs of arousal like a fast heart rate. Researchers are honest that these are small studies, that some of the calming effect may simply come from lying still with your eyes closed, and that larger trials are needed. We share it as interesting — not as a promise.

The Wider World

Other tools people are exploring

Sound beds are one of many tools in the modern wellness space. Here's a simple, honest look at a few others — what each one is and how people commonly use it. This is background information, not medical advice, and none is a treatment for any condition.

🔴

Red light therapy

Panels or beds that give off red and near-infrared light. People use it as part of a relaxation or skin-and-recovery routine. Research varies a lot by use and device — an area still being studied.

🧲

PEMF mats

"Pulsed electromagnetic field" mats deliver gentle, low-level pulses; people lie on them to relax and wind down. The science is early and mixed — an emerging wellness tool, not a proven remedy.

♨️

Infrared saunas

Warm the body directly with infrared heat at gentler temperatures than a traditional sauna. Many people simply enjoy the warmth as a relaxing part of self-care. Specific effects are still being researched.

🌬️

Breathwork tech

Simple devices and apps that pace slow breathing — a handheld tool that expands in your palm, or a coach for techniques like box breathing. Screen-optional ways to slow down.

📈

Biofeedback & wearables

Devices that show what's happening inside — heart-rate variability, brain activity, breathing pace — so you can notice it and adjust. Popular as feedback tools for a meditation practice.

💧

Float tanks

Warm, heavily salted water lets you float effortlessly in a quiet, dark space. People use floating to disconnect from stimulation and rest — valued for the deeply quiet experience.

The Differentiator

Where AI meets relaxation

The newest chapter is artificial intelligence. Instead of a fixed playlist or a one-size-fits-all meditation, AI can build sound and guidance that adapts to you in real time — responding to things like your heart rate, the time of day, even the weather. The idea is a more personal experience that shifts with how you're actually feeling in the moment.

Real examples today

Endel creates soundscapes that adjust in real time to inputs like heart rate and time of day. Somnee uses an AI-driven headband to personalize a wind-down routine and learn over time. A growing number of apps now generate custom ambient music on the fly, tuning it to your preferences and biofeedback.

Our honest take

AI-guided wellness audio is genuinely exciting — and it's early. The market is growing fast, but adaptive, biofeedback-driven relaxation is still a young field and independent research is limited. We'll keep watching it and share what we learn — the promising parts and the open questions alike.
